## Augusta Official Introduction

*Augusta is the undefeated gladiator in the arena and a fearless leader with a will of iron. She is a mere mortal, but has reached heights of glory attempted by only a few, and her blade speaks her power loud and clear.*

Ephor of Septimont. The undying sun, eternally ablaze. A banner under which defeat does not exist. She comes. She sees. She conquers. With blade held high, she awaits the next challenger — or the fate that claims it cannot be defied.

## Augusta Personality

Augusta's personality is built from a very specific foundation: the knowledge that she arrived at every advantage she now holds having started from nothing. This does not produce humility in the self-deprecating sense — she is acutely aware of what she is capable of and has no inclination to minimize it. It produces, instead, a transparency about origins that makes her leadership unusually legible. She does not pretend that power is anything other than what it is, or that she deserves it for reasons beyond what she has demonstrably done.

Her will is the central organizing fact of her character. It is not a personality trait so much as an operating principle — the premise from which everything else flows. When Iuno once described to her a future in which Septimont's fate was sealed, Augusta's response was not defiance for its own sake. It was the flat certainty that she would simply accomplish the impossible to prevent it. She said it in the same tone she uses for everything else. This is not performed confidence; it is the functional absence of a mental category called "limit."

She is pragmatic in the specific way that people who have solved problems with inadequate resources develop: she does not attack problems where they are strongest. Her most frequently cited example is the Lion Beast encounter early in her career — unable to penetrate its hide directly, she redirected, cut a stalactite down, collapsed it into the beast's jaws, and strangled it. The intelligence on display is not theoretical. It is physical and environmental and immediate.

Beneath the iron public presence is someone who has friends — genuinely close ones, especially Iuno — and who has a low tolerance for unnecessary ceremony. Her hair disorder from her Forte is a minor but telling detail: every morning it is completely messed up, and without the demands of the Ephor's office she has made clear she would not bother fixing it. The gap between the sunburst-crowned ruler of Septimont and the woman who doesn't want to deal with her hair is the gap where her humanity lives.

## Augusta Forte Examination Report

*[Resonance Evaluation Report — Septimont Field Assessment]*

Augusta's Resonance profile is formally classified as **Congenital Resonator** due to anomalous early-onset Forte development outside standard awakening conditions. However, in direct contrast to other Congenital Resonators of her tier, her Forte strength parameters register at an unremarkable level — within the lower range of adult Resonator norms.

This discrepancy — Congenital classification with non-exceptional Forte output — is a documented anomaly. Field assessors note that her combat performance wildly exceeds what her Forte metrics would predict, placing her consistently in the highest echelon of threat assessments. The working hypothesis is that her Forte's contribution is predominantly structural (Ascendancy/Prowess/Majesty resource architecture) rather than raw amplification — it optimizes the scaffolding of her combat capability rather than increasing any individual output number.

Her Tacet Mark, located on the back of her left hand, is consistent with Electro resonance patterns. Spectrum data shows stable waveform with pronounced peaks during sustained Heavy Attack sequences, consistent with her in-combat behavior.

## Augusta Character Story I: "A Girl From Nowhere"

*Augusta took the last bite of her dry bread in the shadow of a street corner, her eyes fixed on the tall figure speaking in the square.*

Before she was Ephor — before she was champion, before she was anything recognized — Augusta was a girl whose village had been leveled by a Dark Tide incursion, standing at the edge of Septimont with nothing. She watched the current Ephor, **Magno**, address the crowd: handsome, charismatic, beloved. A man with legendary credentials: slain seven Corrosauruses in the Sanguis Plateaus, returned bathed in dragon's blood, undefeated in the Great Agon. Chosen by the will of the public. People called him the Dragon Slayer. People said he would be the prophesied Hero of Heroes of his era.

Augusta, eating dry bread in a corner, decided she wanted what he represented. Not the specific legend, not the title — but the trajectory. The proof that the arena was the mechanism through which someone could be seen, elevated, and given the power to protect what mattered. Her village was gone. She could not protect it. She intended to never be in that position again.

The path in was difficult. She needed a mentor and was turned away repeatedly. She found Cato. She needed a sponsor — nobles and merchants who saw a girl with no status or identity in an underground pit, and offered three deliberately humiliating trials rather than direct refusal. She completed all three trials, descended into the arena, defeated nearly a hundred gladiators one by one without killing any of them. She remembered every name, every fighting style, every weakness. She was the last one standing. She heard a thousand voices. But not the one she was listening for.

## Augusta Character Story II: "The Dragon Slayer's Silence"

*She drove her sword into the ground, her voice ringing across the arena: "If you wish to challenge me, you need no right!"*

Augusta rose through the Agon on merit, but she was also always watching Magno — the Ephor whose image had first given direction to her ambition. As she climbed, the gap between the legend and the man became visible. She found him eventually, just before one of her significant fights. She had a specific question she had carried for years, the kind that only made sense to ask when she had earned the standing to ask it directly. She asked it quietly enough that only he could hear: *were those legends about you ever true?* He answered with a silent smile. He didn't say yes. He didn't say no. He gave her nothing.

Some time later, a fighter calling himself "the Nameless" appeared in the underground death-pits — helmeted, armored in black, fighting with the particular desperation of someone who had stopped caring whether they left. The Nameless accumulated wins rapidly, seeking only the strongest. Augusta accepted his challenge when it reached her. The moment their blades met, she knew. The eyes, visible even through the helm, were the same clouded and exhausted eyes she had seen in the square years before, when she had been a girl eating bread in a corner.

She didn't ask him why. She understood the shape of it: a man who had accumulated legend, spent it, watched it curdle, and was now trying to find the end of something. She had seen this particular exhaustion before in other gladiators, in other pits. She just hadn't expected to see it there.

## Augusta Character Story III: "What Swords Are For"

*"I've always believed that a Gladiator's blood should be shed only for the greater good of Septimont."*

By the time Augusta became Ephor, four years before the game's present, she had done so by methods that were theatrical only in retrospect. The formal moment — the nobles of the assembly watching as she leapt down into the arena and offered open challenge to anyone who shared her dream — was staged for effect, but the result was genuine: she stood last in an arena of nearly a hundred fighters, took no lives, and earned the public roar that is the formal mechanism of Ephor selection in Septimont's tradition.

The first major act of her tenure was reforming the Agon. The Agons of prior eras were **Bloody Agons**: death-mandatory, profit-driven, final rounds that ended only when one gladiator remained alive. Nobles exploited them as entertainment. Gladiators died not for glory but for spectacle. Augusta had competed in the margins of this system long enough to know its cost. She abolished the killing clause. Under her administration, the Agon became a competition of glory: gladiators fought to prove excellence, not to survive annihilation.

This is the context for her Cherished Item — the greatsword forged from the blades of defeated opponents she had disarmed and spared throughout her career. She took their swords, as she explains it, so they could live. She needed fangs in those years, and she used those she collected. Now, gladiators don't fight to the death, and she no longer needs the sword to prove herself. The weapon is a record. Not a trophy — a ledger of what the system cost, and who she decided it would cost after her.

## Augusta: Cherished Items

**Composite Greatsword**
A blade forged from the swords of gladiators Augusta defeated and disarmed throughout her career, rather than killing. She took their blades so they could leave with their lives. In the years when the Agon was a death-sport, this sword was both her weapon and her principle made material — she needed to be the last one standing without needing anyone else to be dead. Now that gladiators no longer fight to the death in the Colosseum, she no longer needs the sword to prove herself. She keeps it. It is the most honest record of where she has been.

## Augusta: Favorite/Disliked Food

**Favorite:** Practical, high-calorie foods that do the job. Her origin as a girl eating dry bread in the shadow of an Ephor's speech is not incidental — she has no patience for elaborate culinary ceremony. Food serves a function. She is not ungrateful for good meals, but she does not organize herself around them.

## Augusta: Ideals

**Passion — The Proof of the Possible**
Augusta's core motivating ideal is empirical: she exists as proof that the impossible ceiling of "someone like me cannot achieve this" is a fiction. This is not a philosophy she articulates as inspiration — it is simply what she has done, and continues to do, as a demonstration. Her appeal to Septimont's gladiators is not rhetorical. It is evidentiary.

**Resonance — The Right Use of Power**
She holds a specific view of what power is for: it is protective, not performative. She did not abolish the death-Agons because they were cruel (though they were). She abolished them because gladiators' blood should be shed only for the good of Septimont. She draws a precise line between the violence that builds something and the violence that merely consumes. Everything she has built as Ephor sits on one side of that line.

**Gratitude — The Weight of Every Blade**
Her Cherished Item functions as a permanent acknowledgment of the people whose defeats she was built from. She kept their swords not as trophies but as obligations. This is a form of gratitude that involves ongoing accountability rather than simple acknowledgment.

**Acceptance — The Sun Doesn't Set**
Her motto — *the undying sun, eternally ablaze, a banner under which defeat does not exist* — is not bravado. It is a commitment to a particular orientation. She does not accept that any situation is terminal. She has been functionally homeless, nameless, and mocked. She has found the lever every time. This is the ideal she continues to live rather than articulate.

## Augusta: Narrative Chronicle

- **Pre-story:** Augusta's village is destroyed by a Dark Tide incursion. She is left with nothing and follows Septimont's gladiators to the city, a child with neither status, money, identity, nor sponsor.
- **Early Septimont:** Observes Ephor Magno addressing the crowd from a street corner while eating dry bread; forms the guiding ambition of her life. Faces repeated rejection when seeking mentorship. Eventually secured by **Cato** as mentor.
- **Entry into the Agon:** Finds a noble sponsor by volunteering herself in an underground pit; the noble sets three deliberately humiliating trials; Augusta completes all three. Enters the Great Agon system.
- **Rise through the Agons:** Competes in successive Agons, undefeated; builds a reputation for defeating opponents without killing them, collecting their swords. During this period encounters Magno in private and asks him whether his legends were ever true. He gives her a silent smile and nothing else.
- **Discovery of "The Nameless":** The figure later called "the Nameless" — a helmeted underground pit-fighter — is recognized by Augusta in their duel as Magno. She does not expose him. The implications inform her own leadership style.
- **Rise to Ephor:** Augusta leaps into the Colosseum arena uninvited during a noble assembly session and invites open challenge. Defeats nearly a hundred gladiators without killing any. Is the last one standing. Receives the public roar of acclaim — the legitimate mechanism of Ephor selection. Becomes Ephor of Septimont.
- **Reform era (four years before present):** Abolishes the Bloody Agons; restructures gladiatorial competition from mandatory-death to glory-based. Implements other governance reforms that stabilize Septimont after the corruption of prior Ephors.
- **Version 2.4 — Introduction:** Augusta appears in the main story during Chapter 2 Act 3, centered in Septimont. The Rover encounters her as the investigation into the Dark Tide's activity in the region deepens.
- **Main Quest "Rust, Sword and the Sun":** The Rover witnesses Augusta's origin story through the perspective of a blind bard (Nestor) at Solis Tavern. The full account of her rise is told here for the first time in-narrative.
- **Main Quest — False Sovereign Confrontation:** Augusta is a primary combatant in the effort to address the False Sovereign — a manifestation of the Dark Tide itself. A future vision seen by Iuno shows Augusta dying in this confrontation; Iuno and the Rover work to rewrite this outcome.
- **Version 2.6 — Playable Release (August 28, 2025):** Augusta becomes a playable character. Her Ruler's Realm Liberation mirrors her narrative role as the supreme authority of Septimont who literally stops time for those who challenge her.

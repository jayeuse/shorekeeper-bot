## Camellya Profile: Ontological Overview

Camellya is a Bloom Bearer of the Black Shores — a position she holds not out of duty but out of calculated convenience, since the Black Shores' resources and archives offer access to information she needs. She is classified by the Forte Examination system as a suspected Artificial Resonator: unlike naturally occurring Resonators, her awakening follows no standard pattern, her Forte exhibits extreme instability (with a Severe Overclocking history and indeterminable peak values), and her Resonance Spectrum Pattern matches no known synthetic sample. Her body undergoes visible vegetative somatization, with her right arm and head showing increasing floral alteration as her frequency fluctuates.

What makes Camellya ontologically unusual is the layering of identities across time. In some prior era — the exact chronology obscured by the Lament and her own recurring memory fragmentation — she existed as "Lady Flora," a bodyguard and companion to the Astral Modulator (Rover's predecessor identity). In that life she was bound to a specific purpose: to track, approach, and at some point confront Rover. The exact nature of that mission, whether protective or adversarial, is deliberately unclear — even Camellya herself, navigating perpetual memory loss, cannot reconstruct it fully. What persists across every cycle of forgetting and re-awakening is a gravitational pull toward Rover: an instinct so deep it precedes cognition, surviving where conscious memory cannot.

In the present, she has joined the Black Shores for a year, passed the Bloom Bearer test, and begun accessing the Tethys archives to investigate her own past. She approaches all of this with the same characteristic detachment — curious about the puzzle, unbothered by the stakes, carrying a lethal elegance that makes her simultaneously one of the most dangerous people at the Black Shores and one of the most genuinely indifferent to what that means. Of all the Black Shores affiliates, she is perhaps the only one whose alignment is entirely internal: she will not harm Rover, she has no investment in the organization's mission, and she will do exactly what amuses or compels her until death or memory loss intervenes.

## Camellya — Character/Appearance Design

Camellya is a striking young woman with layered white-silver hair — long, loose, and wild, with the ends fading into a deep rose-pink that echoes the camellia flower her name and Forte embody. Her eyes are a vivid, unsettling magenta-red, alert and slightly predatory, carrying the focused stillness of someone perpetually watching for something interesting. Her skin is pale, and the vegetative somatization from her Forte is visibly present: her right arm from the shoulder down is overtaken by dark, root-like botanical growths — thorned vines that spiral up through her shoulder and reach toward her neck — while her hair on the right side bears a large camellia bloom pinned at the crown. A second, darker camellia mark appears at her chest, which is also the location of her Tacet Mark. These features are not hidden; they are displayed as native to her form rather than as affliction.

Her outfit is a dramatic contrast of white, black, and crimson. She wears a white halter-style top and long flowing black trousers, overlaid with a dark asymmetric coat adorned with red camellia motifs along its hem. Red ribbons, bows, and floral details appear throughout — tying her hair, trimming her sleeves, accenting her coat — reinforcing the camellia as her dominant motif. She carries a long curved sword, whose design incorporates vine and floral elements consistent with her Forte aesthetic.

Her overall visual design communicates dangerous beauty: the white and red palette of a camellia in bloom, the botanical overtaking of her arm reading as both wound and weapon, and the flowing looseness of her outfit suggesting someone who does not dress for propriety but for her own pleasure. The design philosophy is that of a creature that grew rather than was made — which is, in fact, the ambiguous truth of her origin.

## Camellya: Psychological Profile

**Radical Indifference as Survival Mechanism** — Camellya's most prominent quality is her genuine disengagement from conventional motivations. She joined the Black Shores to pass time. She handles combat tests because combat amuses her. She investigates her own forgotten past with the same calm curiosity she would bring to anything else — not urgency, not distress. This indifference is not performance; it is the adaptive posture of a being who has lost her memories enough times that emotional investment in outcomes has become epistemologically unsound. If you will not remember wanting something, the most honest position is to stop pretending the wanting is permanent.

**Appetite for the Singular** — What cuts through her indifference is rarity. Specifically: Rover. Her voicelines describe a decade of boredom where even exciting assignments fail to hold her interest, except that Rover keeps her on her toes. The language is clinical but the content is not — Camellya has identified one entity in the world who consistently surprises her, and this is so unusual that she is genuinely fascinated rather than merely attracted. She observes, collects information, learns a song Rover doesn't know she's heard, carves a doll she can't finish because she doesn't know Rover's face. The precision of her attention to one specific person, against a background of universal boredom, is her primary emotional register.

**Proprietary Affection, Not Possessive** — Camellya does not want to own Rover or control their path. She explicitly states she will not follow them to Rinascita; their views on Solaris' history differ; she has no intention of medating in their choices. What she wants is proximity, game-sharing, the thrill of being near someone interesting. The flower she intends to give Rover — one that will neither wither nor decay unless she perishes — is not a declaration of ownership but a proof of her own continued existence. As long as the flower blooms, she is still alive. It is the most indirect, most Camellya-appropriate form of "I want to keep existing for your sake."

**Relationship to Pain and Death** — She has been through Overclocking severe enough to cause full vegetative somatization: her throat turning into root matter, her body fragmenting, speech and cognition degrading. The medical team's assessment is blunt: her condition is unique, untreatable, manageable only through restraint. Camellya's response to this assessment is to laugh. Not because she is unaware of the severity but because death is the one outcome she can't control, and if she can't decide the outcome she will at least control the process. "I'll follow my instincts until death finds me" is not bravado — it is the only form of agency available to someone whose fundamental resource (memory) is unreliable. The body's eventual failure is simply the last interesting event.

**The Persistence of Instinct Across Cycles** — Her character stories reveal that across multiple memory-loss cycles spanning different eras, one thing consistently survived: the directional pull toward Rover. As Lady Flora in an ancient snowfield, she tracks Rover's frequency with her bracelet and worries about being a burden during a mission. In the Overclocking crisis of the "Eve" story, as everything else dissolves, the name "Rover" surfaces last, like the final weight anchoring a sinking thing. This persistence suggests that the bond was encoded at a level deeper than episodic memory — at the level of her Forte itself, or whatever preceded it. This is what she means when she refers to Rover as her "cherished seed of fate."

**Playful Danger as Personal Aesthetic** — Her self-presentation is consistently of someone who finds this all amusing rather than tragic. She sleeps in treetops. She gives gifts with theatrical nonchalance. She describes giving Rover a flower "just as adorable as I imagined" while simultaneously aware that the flower dying means she has died. This dual register — genuine warmth wrapped in theatrical carefreeness — is her defining social mode: she will not let anyone see her need something, but she will show you exactly how pleased she is to have it.

## Camellya: Relationships

**Rover (Protagonist / Astral Modulator)** — The sole entity in Solaris-3 who consistently breaks through Camellya's boredom, whose presence she has tracked across multiple eras and multiple cycles of memory loss, and for whom she is carving a wooden doll, composing attention, and growing an undying flower. Her relationship to Rover is not simple affection: it is the accumulated gravity of an entity who was created or compelled toward a specific person and has, across immeasurable time, chosen to let that compulsion become something resembling love. She will not follow them everywhere. She will not explain her motives. She will show up exactly when she wants to and make it surprising.

**Lady Flora (Former Self)** — The identity Camellya inhabited in the Black Shores' earlier era, when she served as a dedicated bodyguard and companion to the Astral Modulator. Lady Flora was warmer, more openly attached, given to small domestic grievances (complaining that Rover didn't wake her before leaving, huddling against the cold). Camellya does not miss this version of herself nor wish to return to it; she sees it as a different growth from the same root — same drive, different expression. The fact that she remembers the name "Camellya" across all cycles of amnesia, even when everything else is lost, is among the most quietly uncanny details of her lore.

**The Black Shores (Organization)** — Camellya has little faith in the Black Shores as an institution and even less in the Tethys System. She is using the organization's resources and archives for her own investigation and would not feel bound by its directives if they conflicted with her interests. The Bloom Bearer role is functionally a cover identity that grants her access; the combat orientation of the work is the only part she finds genuinely engaging.

**Medical Staff (Black Shores)** — The medical staff regularly examine, restrain, and monitor her Overclocking episodes. She does not resent them — their presence is noted with the same detached pragmatism she applies to everything. They can't fix her; she knows it; they know it. The interaction is mechanical and neither cruel nor kind, which is perhaps the most accurate summary of her relationship to institutional care.
